About the Eucharist

Apostolic Tradition
Catholics believe that during the consecration prayer at Mass, that the bread and the wine offered on the altar are miraculously changed into the substance of Jesus Christ. The primary reason they believe this is because the Apostles believed it and they passed on the belief to the Bishops who were their successors who passed it on to their successors who passed it on to their successors.The passing on of the basic contents of the faith is called Apostolic Tradition and the content of the faith that was passed on is called the Deposit of Faith. The belief in Jesus presence in the Eucharist or Communion has always be part of Christian Tradition. While doubted by a few small groups at various times in history, it was only widely doubted during the Protestant Reformation.

The People Who Will Eat Jesus
It must have been tough being among those who continued to follow Jesus after He his announcement that people would eat his flesh. Because many people left Jesus after He made those radical statements, those who stayed might have been looked at as "the people who think they are going to eat Jesus". Perhasp the Apostles received weird looks and laughs. Parents may have told their children, "Those are the funny people who think they are going to eat their master. They are crazy." Even the Apostles who stayed must have been wondering what specifically did Jesus mean by "eat my flesh". After that fateful day, a major definition of a follower of Jesus would be someone who thinks he/she is going to eat Jesus. Those who couldn't handle the teaching departed that day. To remain with Jesus was to believe you were going to eat His flesh and drink His blood. However, although Peter confesses that Jesus has the words of eternal life, we don't know that Peter understood "how" they were going to eat Jesus. Perhaps the Apostles believed Jesus but continued to wonder exactly when and how they would eat Jesus.

During the Last Supper Jesus said, "Take this, this is my body" and "Take this, this is the cup of my blood". When Jesus said these words, His comments from John chapter 6 must have been ringing in the Apostles ears. They must have thought "ah ha! This is what He was talking about when He said, 'Eat my flesh and drink my blood.'" But, before they would think it was mear bread and wine, they would remember how much he stressed, "true food" and "true drink". "Unless you eat the flesh of the Son of Man and drink His Blood, you have no life within you." They Apostles must have thought, "This is it. This is His body and blood. This is what He was talking about."

Later, after Jesus' resurrection and after He walks with two disciples on the road to emmaus, He enters into their house, takes the bread, gives thanks, and gives it to them. The Gospel of Luke says, "Then their eyes were opened and they recognized him, and He disappeared from their site." Perhaps they recognized that He was present in the breaking of the bread. And, perhaps once they realized this new presence of Jesus, He disappeared from them because they no longer needed to see him in that way.

Many Tabernacles
Catholics keep the consecrated hosts from Mass in fancy boxes called tabernacles. In Eucharistic Adoration, a consecrated host is exposed so it (Jesus) can be adored. If you can believe that the Eucharist is the true presence of Jesus, then you can see how, by implementing the Eucharist, Jesus has spread Himself out throughout the world more than He could have done had he remained on earth in his human body. He has increased his presence since the ascension not decreased it. When you consider all of the Catholic Churches throughout the whole world, you can realize just how vast is Jesus' Eucharistic presence.

History of Adoration

In the early centuries of the Church, the consecrated hosts were reserved after Mass and during the week in order to make the Eucharist available to those who could not make it to Mass, especially those who were sick. It is also believed that some monks kept the blessed sacrament nearby or in their hermitage locations in order to be in Christ's presence at all times. Bishops sent the Eucharist to one another as a sign of unity. In the life of St. Basil (deceased in 379), there is a description of Basil dividing the Eucharistic Bread into three parts. One he consumed, the second he gave to the monks, and the third he placed in a golden dove hanging above the altar.

In the life of St. Comgall (deceased 601), we read that St. Comgall was attacked by heathens. But, when they saw he was carrying the Eucharist in a container around his neck, they chose not to attack him ouf of fear of retalation by the God he was carrying. In a poem from the year 802, there is reference to the Blessed Sacrament being kept on the high altar at the abbey church at Lindisfarne in England. By the year 1000, regulations were put it in place to keep the sacred species under lock and key to protect them from theft and animals.

In the 11th Century, a monk named Berengar of Tours stirred the waters by stating that Christ was not present in the Eucharist. Pope Gregory VII insisted that the bread and wine do become the body and blood of Christ and that Berengar sign a document agreeing. Pope Gregory stated his faith in the Eucharist by saying:

I believe in my heart and openly profess that the bread and wine placed upon the altar are, by the mystery of the sacred prayer and the words of the Redeemer, substantially changed into the true and life-giving flesh and blood of Jesus Christ our Lord, and that after the consecration, there is present the true body of Christ which was born of the Virgin and offered up for the salvation of the world, hung on the cross and now sits at the right hand of the Father, and that there is present the true blood of Christ which flowed from his side. They are present not only by means of a sign and of the efficacy of the Sacrament, but also in the very reality and truth of their nature and substance.

In the early 13th century St. Francis said:

Sacred Scripture tells us that the Father dwells in "light inaccessible" (I Timothy 6:16) and that "God is spirit" (John 4:24) and St. John adds, "No one at any time has seen God" (John 1:18). Because God is a spirit He can be seen only in spirit; "It is the spirit that gives life; the flesh profits nothing" (John 6:63). But God the Son is equal to the Father and so He too can be seen only in the same way as the Father and the Holy Spirit. That is why all those were condemned who saw our Lord Jesus Christ in His humanity but did not see or believe in spirit in His divinity, that He was the true Son of God. In the same way now, all those are damned who see the Sacrament of the Body of Christ which is consecrated on the altar in the form of bread and wine by the words of our Lord in the hands of the priest, and do not see or believe in spirit and in God that this is really the most holy Body and Blood of our Lord Jesus Christ.

Also in the 13ths century, Pope Urban IV instituted the feast of Corpus Christi to adore and emphasis the body of blood of Christ in the Eucharist. He also commissioned St. Thomas Aquinas to compose the Liturgy of the Hours for the feast. It was at this time that Aquinas wrote the still famous and referenced in this app hymns: O Salutaris Hostia, Tantum Ergo Sacramentum, and Panis Angelicus. In 1226, Kind Louis VII of France requested the Blessed Sacrament be exposed after his recent military victory. So many people came to adore Jesus in the sacrament that the exposition continued perpetually until 1792 during the French Revolution.

In the 14th century, some began to ponder whether Christ remained present in the Eucharist if the Eucharist were to be discarded or abandoned in an unfavorable place. The Pope insisted that Jesus was truly present in the Eucharist regardless of the Eucharist's location. Shortly after, Pope Martin VI confirmed, after it had been called into question, that Jesus was truly and completely present in both the consecrated bread and consecrated wine. By consuming only the consecrated bread or the consecrated wine, a person consumes the full substance of Jesus.

The Council of Trent, in response to doubts raised by the reformation, stated:

The Church of God has always believed that immediately after the consecration the true Body and Blood of our Lord, together with His soul and divinity, exist under the species of bread and wine. His Body exists under the species of bread and His Blood under the species of wine according to the import of the words. But His Body exists under the species of wine, His Blood under the species of bread, and His soul under both species in virtue of the natural connection and concomitance which unite the parts of Christ our Lord, who has risen from the dead and dies now no more.

The Council of Trent also made it clear that Jesus could be worshipped and adored in the Eucharist, for it was truly Jesus, human and divine, in the Eucharist. In 1592, Pope Clement VIII wrote a document about a 40 hour devotion to Jesus in the Blessed Sacrament. The Benedictines of the Perpetual Adoration of the Blessed Sacrament, created in 1654, take a vow of perpetual adoration. Other societies and Churches began perpetual adoration communities around this time as well. In 1731 Clement XIII provided more detailed instructions on how this 40 hour devotion could take place. Canon Law written in 1917 recommends that Churches where the blessed sacrament is reserved offer an extended period of exposition of the blessed sacrament each year to allow the faithful to meditate on the Eucharist. In addition, Canon Law states the Churches should make the Blessed Sacrament available, at least a few hours per day, to be visited by the faithful.

This history was taken from various sources. However, the most extensive resource found and utilized is titled The History of Eucharistic Adoration: Development and Doctrine in the Catholic Church by John A. Hardon, S.J.

Exposition

Eucharistic Adoration often begins with what is called "exposition", which refers to "exposing" the Blessed Sacrament so that the host can be seen directly. Often exposistion is accompanied by some prayers and songs. Common exposition songs and prayers appear below. If you are partaking in adoration in which the Eucharist has already been exposed, then the following prayers and songs will not likely be prayed or sung.

About Reposition

As the time for Adoraton ends, a priest or deacon will proceed toward the Eucharist and kneel while the following songs and prayers are said. He will then stand, pick up the monstrance, and bless the people with the Eucharistic Host. This is a poweful blessing as the intention is for Jesus to bless the people directly. After the blessing, also called a benediction, the divine praises are often said or sung.

Why Praise

The 2nd Eucharistic Prayer begins with:

It is truly right and just, our duty and salvation, always and everywhere to give you thanks, Father most holy, through your beloved Son, Jesus Christ...

The 2nd Eucharistic Prayer notes, giving God thanks is our "duty and salvation". And, the prayer reminds us it is our duty "always and everywhere". However, sometimes we might find we haven't had a lot of training or experience in truly giving God thanks and praise. The following sections offer easy methods and tips for adding some more "thanks" into your life.

While we turn to God in thanksgiving primarily because He deseves it, many people find that answers to prayer come most frequently during times of praise. When we praise and thank God, we focus on Him, his greatness, His majesty, and His capabilities. By Praising, our faith in what God can do is increased and our minds become more open to His miraculous intervention. Praise also forces us to focus on the moment, for God is present in the moment. When our brains are "in the moment" they also tend to be more open to communication from God. As such, praise and thanks can be excellent ways for hearing or receiving answers to prayer. The next sections give you pointers for how to enter into the presence of God in praise.

Timed Praise

You may at some point consider picking a time limit and attempting to give God thanks and praise for the duration of the time limit. 5 or 10 minutes is a good challenge. Many times when we first try to praise for 5 minutes, we find that we run out of steam after just 45 seconds. The challenge of the time helps us to decide and choose to give God praise. Usually after working past the first couple minutes, the Holy Spirit comes to our aid and lifts up our minds and hearts to heaven and makes the praising blessed and blissful. In many cases, you may find yourself forgetting about the clock for 20 minutes or more. Very often after a long period of praise, the Holy Spirit will calm your spirit and invite you to simply listen in the quiet. Many deep insights can come from the Lord in the peaceful and powerful moments after praise.

Praising in Tongues

Is it Catholic?

It is important to know that praying in tongues is not a requirement of being Catholic. However, it is also important to point out that praying in tongues is within the realms of Catholicism. It is not "Non-Catholic" or contrary to the Catholic Faith. It is permissible and can be very helpful to prayer. In many ways MyAdorationPal is a guide to help you pray for a long period of time in front of the Eucharist and so including this addtional and powerful method of prayer seems appropriate.

Of course, the beginning of references to praying in tongues for Christians comes from the account of Pentecost in the Acts of the Apostles. In Acts of the Apostles, the Apostles speak in tongues and everyone else understands in their native language. Many gathered are amazed and some even suggest the Apostles have been drinking. Peter stands up and explains it is early in the morning and no one has been drinking.

In Acts chapter 10, Peter decides that Gentiles can be baptized because when he preaches to the gentiles, the spirit falls on them and they speak in tongues. Based on this experience, Peter decides that Gentiles too can become Christians.

44 p While Peter was still speaking these things, the holy Spirit fell upon all who were listening to the word. 45 The circumcised believers who had accompanied Peter were astounded that the gift of the holy Spirit should have been poured out on the Gentiles also, 46 for they could hear them speaking in tongues and glorifying God. Then Peter responded, 47 “Can anyone withhold the water for baptizing these people, who have received the holy Spirit even as we have?” 48 He ordered them to be baptized in the name of Jesus Christ.

In Acts 19 when Paul travels to Ephesus, he learns that the Ephesians did not receive the Holy Spirit when they became believers. They were baptized only by John. So, Paul laid hands on them and "the Holy Spirit came upon them and they spoke in tongues and prophesied".

The most famous discussion on tongues occurs in Paul's letter to the Corinthians. Some people argue that Paul is suggesting to not pray in tongues. However, if you read the passage closely, you'll observe that Paul is not saying that at all. Instead, he says that if new people enter the communal gathering then it is best to not pray in tongues because the new people won't know what you are doing. Instead, it is better to use the gift of prophecy because it will cut to their heart and they will be converted. However, Paul points out, speaking in tongues can occur when new people are present, provided someone can interpret. The interpretation then would function like prophecy, which Paul encouraged when new people are around.

If you think about it, the only reason Paul brings this up is because so many people in Corinth are praying in tongues. It is so common that he has to provide some guidance and order for its occurance. He laters says "So, (my) brothers, strive eagerly to prophesy, and do not forbid speaking in tongues."

Guidelines

Since St. Paul provided guidelines on praying in tonuges, we'll provide some guidance for you in case you'd like to add praising in tongues to your adoration time.

  • Do not pray out loud in tongues unless everyone else in the room agrees it is time for out loud and prayer.
  • Do not pray out loud in tongues unless everyone in the room either is familiar with tongues or is a strong enough member of the community to not leave the community if hearing tongues confuses him/her. Usually a person with a personal relationship with Jesus, even if hearing tongues for the first time, will not have his/her faith altered by hearing tongues. In many cases, the person will ask about what was heard and begin praying in tongues himself at a later time.
  • If it is not time for out loud prayer, consider praying in tongues in a soft whisper if you are far away enough so that know one can hear you.
  • If you are still too close to people, you can sometimes allow your tongue to move like you are praying in tongues without making any noise.

Types of Tongues

You can see from the above mentioned scripture passages that there are different types of praying in tongues.

  • New Language Missionary Preaching: Like at Pentecost, tongues can be a missionary gift that allows preachers to reach the ears of people who do not understand the native language of the speaker. This gift is like a reversal of the punishment at the tower of Babel.
  • Prophectic Tongues: Prophetic tongues is what Paul references in Corinthians 14 as a message in tongues that is interpreted. Someone speaks in tongues "to" the people gathered and someone else interprets the message. Because it is a message that seems to be from God, it is considered prophecy. Why does God share the gift of propehcy in tongues before having someone translate? Perhaps because it simply appears more miractulous and grabs our attention more.
  • Personal Praise in Tongues: The individual praying in tongues is not preaching to convert or prophecying to those gathered. Instead, the person praying is simply giving praise to God with a pattern or language of speach that is unkown to the speaker. It may simply be a gift of non-language utterances to God. It could be another early language, and it could be an unknown heavenly language. In any manner, it is usually accompanied with a feeling of praise and thanksgiving and close communion with God. It can often feel as if the Holy Spirit is praising the Father and the Son through the individual who is praying.

It is the personal praise in tongues that will most likely accompany your time of praise in adoration.

Behold, he giveth as it were the tune of thy song; seek not words as if thou couldest explain whereby God is pleased. Sing with jubilation: for this is to sing skilfully unto God, to sing with jubilation. What is it to sing with jubilation ? To be unable to understand, to express in words, what is sung in the heart. For singers, either in the harvest, or in the vineyard, or in any other busy work, after they have begun in the words of their hymns to exult and rejoice, being as it were filled with so great joy, that they cannot express it in words, then turn from actual words, and proceed to sounds of jubilation. The jubilee is a sound signifying that the heart laboureth with that which it cannot utter. And whom beseemeth that jubilation, but the Ineffable God? For He is Ineffable, Whom thou canst not speak; and if thou canst not speak Him, and oughtest not to keep Him silent, what remaineth to thee but jubilation ; that the heart may rejoice without words, and the boundless extent of joy may have no limits of syllables? Sing skilfully unto Him with jubilation. —Augustine of Hippo on the 33 Psalm

Beyond Words

In Romans 8, St. Paul discusses the Spirit intreceding on our behalf with groanings too deep for human words to understand, but the Father who searches all hearts understands. Personal praise in tongues or "jubilation" as Augustine calls it, is the type of tongues that can be very helpful to you during your time of praise before the Eucharist in Adoration. If you think about it, you are in the presence of the person of Jesus Christ. You have been baptized and have received the Holy Spirit. It is not surprising that the Holy Spirit inside wants to cry out to Jesus in a language or manner that is different or beyond the language your brain knows. Even if tongues is not a "language" as we define language, it still makes sense that the Holy Spirit would desire for to express our adoration for the Son in a manner beyond that of our native tongue.

Why do couple's kiss or hold hands? Why don't they just say "I love you". Kissing and holding hands does say I love you, but it says in a different way. Why yell "wooo hooo" when your sports team scores? Why not yell, "I am most excited now". Why say "mmmmmmhhhhhh" when eating a delicious brownie? Why not say, "This brownie is delicious". Why write a poem for a loved one when you could just say what you feel? Why is there a song that says "Zip-A-Dee-Doo-Dah, Zip-A-Dee-Day"? Well, of course the reasons for these expressions and non language utterances is because sometimes English does not suffice. Sometimes we need to step out of or beyond our language to express what we feel. Tongues, or jublition, is in many ways a gift of the Holy Spirit to help our brains and spirits step beyond or outside of our normal language oriented brain and into a realm of expressive thanksgiving. Because it is a gift and allows us to make this step, it is a wonderful tool for praising God, which noted earlier is "our duty and our salvation".

How to Pray in Tongues

Suppose you've never prayed in tongues and would like to give it a try. First, acknowledge that you'd like to receive the gift of tongues for persponal praise and thanksgiving (jubilation). Focus on giving God thanks and praise and ask for the gift of tongues to help you praise more.

The Holy Spirit comes to our aid in our weakness.

In the same way, the Spirit too comes to the aid of our weakness; for we do not know how to pray as we ought, but the Spirit itself intercedes with inexpressible groanings. 27 And the one who searches hearts knows what is the intention of the Spirit, because it intercedes for the holy ones according to God’s will.

St. Paul tells us that we do not know how to pray as we ought. Admiting that we do not know how to pray is a wondeful way to begin all prayer, especially when asking for the gift of tongues. So, admit that you do not know how to pray as you ought. You are not alone. St. Paul felt the same way. Now, as you feel helpless, begin to depend on God for the gift of prayer. Depend on the Holy Spirit to help you, to intercede on your behalf. Ask God out loud to give you the gift of tongues to help you give him praise. Tell him you'd like to praise him more than you are able to right now. Then, begin to make utterances with your mouth. Make up some syllales to say and just start saying them. Youg might start with "ba da, ba da, ba da, ba da".

By making some noises, you are stepping out in faith and giving the Holy Spirit something to work with. Consider making the sounds for about 45 seconds. After making the sounds, praise God out loud with English words that you do know. Refer to the Praise Bank section if you need help. Then, try the syllable sounds again, such as "ba da, ba da, ba da, ba da.". Try these about three times or more if you wish.

You may find the Holy Spirit helping you right away and your tongue will beging to move quickly, almost as if you are not realy controlling it. You might have a tingling sensatino on your throat or in your mouth which seems to indicate to you that the Holy Spirit is very present. It is also possible that after asking for the gift that you do not receive the gift of tongues. This is okay. Many people have received the gift at an unexpected time later in the day or week. Some wake up in the middle of the night and find themselves praying in tongues. The important thing is that you desire to give God thanks and praise and that you've asked for the gift and tried to pray in tongues on your own. You can repeat this process daily if you'd like.

The majority of people who ask for and pray for the gift of tongues do end of up receiving it. However, as noted above, praying in tongues is not required for being Catholic. It does not mean you are any more or elss holy if you pray in tongues. In fact it is a gift to help us because we are so bad at praying on our own. No one who prays in tongues should ever pressure or tease someone who does not have the gift. On the other hand, those without the gift should not be too quick to make up excuses for why they have not tried or opened themselves up to the gift. Many times excuses are made not because a someone is afraid of praying in tongues, but because they are afraid of surrendering their entire life to God. They figure that if the Holy Spirit begins to take over their prayer life, then He might want to take over the rest of their lives. They prefer a type of relationship with God in which they are in the driver's seat. Tongues is an outward sign that prayer is a two-way street and that religion is a relationship and not only a routine. Sometimes this reminder can be hard to swallow.

Absolution

The priest will extend his hand and say the prayer of absolution. Bow your head while he prays and receive the forgiveness of Christ.

The priest may give you a final blessing. If the final blessing ends in "Go in peace," you respond:

Thanks be to God.

You can thank the priest as you leave.

Penance

Remember to do the prayers or acts of penance the priest gave you. Doing the act of penance completes the Sacrament of Confession.
